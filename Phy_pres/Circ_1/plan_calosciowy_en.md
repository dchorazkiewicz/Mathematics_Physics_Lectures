# Common style for all slides

Use this style consistently for every slide:

**Format:** 16:9
**Language:** English
**Background:** very light, almost white or warm cream
**Style:** clean academic vector infographic
**Text amount:** minimal, only title, short labels, formulas
**Font:** simple sans-serif, large and readable
**Main colors:**

* blue — electrons, electric field, voltage
* red/orange — current, energy, heat
* gray — metal, wires, lattice, circuit elements
* green — correct results, important consequences

**Layout rule:**

* top 12–15%: title
* center 70–75%: main illustration
* bottom 10–15%: one formula, slogan, or key sentence

**Circuit style:**

* thick readable wires
* consistent resistor style
* classic battery symbol
* black dots for nodes
* arrows for currents
* clear (+) and (-) signs where needed
* no dense text blocks

# 1. What Is Electric Current?

## Goal

Introduce electric current as directed flow of electric charge.

## Slide layout

Title at the top left:

**What Is Electric Current?**

In the center, draw a simple horizontal conductor with charges moving through it in one clear direction.

Above the conductor, draw a large blue arrow:

**electric field (E)**

Below the conductor, draw a red/orange arrow:

**conventional current (I)**

At the bottom:

**Ordered motion of electric charges**

## Visual details

Draw the conductor as a gray metallic strip or cylinder. Charges should be blue dots with small minus signs. Use a few clean motion arrows so the idea is simple and readable. Keep this slide general and intuitive.

## Avoid

Do not show random thermal motion yet. Do not show microscopic lattice details. Do not use (I=\frac{dq}{dt}) yet.

# 2. Where Can Current Flow?

## Goal

Show that different media conduct current through different charge carriers.

## Slide layout

Title:

**Where Can Current Flow?**

In the center, use five clean tiles/icons:

1. **Metal — electrons**
2. **Electrolyte — ions**
3. **Gas — ions + electrons**
4. **Vacuum — electron beam**
5. **Semiconductor — electrons + holes**

Arrange them as three tiles on top and two centered below.

At the bottom:

**Different media conduct through different charge carriers**

## Visual details

Metal: wire with small blue electrons.
Electrolyte: two electrodes in liquid with positive and negative ions.
Gas: small discharge with ions and electrons.
Vacuum: electron beam between cathode and anode.
Semiconductor: simple diode or chip symbol with electrons and holes.

## Avoid

Do not redefine electric current. Do not repeat the conductor-with-drift picture from slide 1. This is only an orientation slide about charge carriers.

# 3. Why Do Metals Conduct?

## Goal

## Slide layout

**Why Do Metals Conduct?**

Split the slide into two panels.

Left panel:

**metal lattice**

Draw a regular grid of large gray positive ions.

Right panel:

**free electrons can drift**

Draw the same lattice, but with blue free electrons moving between ions.

At the bottom:

**Positive ion lattice + free electrons**

## Visual details

Positive ions should be gray spheres with (+) signs. Electrons should be small blue dots with (-) signs. Add a blue electric field arrow above the right panel and small red/orange drift arrows on electrons.

## Avoid

Do not make it too realistic. It should look like a clean educational model.

# 4. Random Motion and Drift

## Goal

Show that electron motion is mostly chaotic, but with a small average drift in an electric field.

## Slide layout

Title:

**Random Motion and Drift**

Two panels side by side.

Left panel title:

**No external field**

Right panel title:

**With external field**

Bottom sentence:

**Random motion + small drift = electric current**

## Visual details

Left panel: many blue electrons with short arrows in random directions.

Right panel: many blue electrons still have random arrows, but add a clear average drift direction. Add a large blue arrow labeled (E). Add one small label:

**drift velocity**

## Avoid

Do not show electrons as simply flying straight through the wire. The point is that drift is small compared to chaotic motion.

**The current direction is a convention**

## Goal

Show that collisions transfer energy from moving electrons to the lattice.

**Why Does a Wire Heat Up?**

Middle: collisions with lattice ions.

Right side: conductor becomes warmer, with red/orange glow.

Use arrows:

**electrical energy**

to

**thermal energy**

Bottom:

**Collisions transfer energy to the lattice**

## Visual details
